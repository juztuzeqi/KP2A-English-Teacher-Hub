🧭 How to Use the Phonics Basics Series

A guide for teachers using the Phonics Basics books.

Why Start with CVC, Not the Alphabet?

In English-speaking countries, children learn the alphabet first because they already know the sounds of their language. They just need to connect letters to sounds they already hear.

In Indonesia, English is a foreign language, not a second language. Children do not hear English at home. Starting with the alphabet means teaching 26 abstract names without meaning.

Starting with CVC words (cat, rat, bat, pan, man, van) gives children:

  • Immediate success: they read real words on day one
  • Phonological awareness: they learn to blend and rhyme by changing the first letter
  • Meaningful learning: every letter is attached to a word they already know

The alphabet is still taught — but later, as a tool, not a starting point. Children who already know the alphabet often cannot blend sounds. CVC fixes that quickly.

The goal is not to name letters. The goal is to read words.

Why This Order: Short A → Short I → Short E → Short O → Short U

Short A first

Short A (/æ/) is the most consistent vowel sound in English. It almost always makes the same sound: cat, hat, bat, man, van, sad. There are very few exceptions. This means the student can focus on one clear sound without confusion.

Short A words are also very common and easy to picture: cat, bag, map, apple. The student can see them, draw them, and understand them immediately.

Short I second

Short I (/ɪ/) sounds very different from Short A. The two sounds are far apart in the mouth: /æ/ is open, /ɪ/ is closed. This makes it easy for the student to hear and feel the difference. If we started with Short E (/ɛ/) right after Short A, the student might mix them up because /æ/ and /ɛ/ are close sounds.

Short E third

Short E (/ɛ/) sits between /æ/ and /ɪ/. By now the student already knows two clear, separate vowel sounds. They can compare: "cat" (/æ/), "sit" (/ɪ/), "bed" (/ɛ/). The contrast helps them hear the new sound more easily.

Short O fourth

Short O (/ɒ/ or /ɑ/) introduces a rounded-back vowel. The student now has three front vowels (A, I, E) and one back vowel. This expands their sound map.

Short U last (both sounds)

Short U covers two sounds: /ʌ/ (cup, sun) and /ʊ/ (put, book). These are the most variable and the most difficult for Indonesian learners. We save them for last because by now the student has built confidence with more consistent vowels. They are ready to learn that one letter can make two different sounds — a concept that prepares them for Grade 1 and IPA.

/ʌ/ is taught first because it is the most common sound of "u" in closed syllables and is produced near the centre of the mouth, close to the familiar Short A (/æ/). /ʊ/ is taught later as a bonus because it requires a new motor skill — rounded lips and a higher tongue position. This order also prepares students for a key concept: one letter can make two different sounds.

How to Use These Books

Step 1 – Learn new words

The student learns 3–4 new words per unit.

  • Listen to the pronunciation (teacher or audio).
  • Write each word 5 times (rewriting section).

Step 2 – Match words with pictures

The student draws a line from each word to the correct picture. This tests memory without pressure.

Step 3 – Write under pictures

The student writes the correct word or phrase under each picture. This builds spelling and word recognition.

Step 4 – Let's color (bonus activity)

  • The teacher says a word aloud.
  • The student points to the picture of that word.
  • Then the student colors the pictures as a reward.

Coloring is not just fun — it reinforces listening and vocabulary.

Step 5 – Final Test (per part)

At the end of each part (e.g., Short A Part 1), the student takes a Final Test:

  • 15 pictures with blank lines
  • Student writes the correct word or phrase
  • Passing score: maximum 3 mistakes (75%)
  • If passed → move to the next part
  • If not → review the unit(s) where mistakes were made

Step 6 – Bridging Test (between vowels)

After finishing one vowel (e.g., Short A Part 2) and before starting the next vowel (e.g., Short I), the student takes a Bridging Test:

  • Mix of pictures from the previous vowel
  • 15–20 questions
  • Passing score: maximum 4 mistakes
  • Purpose: strengthen long-term memory and connect vowels

Step 7 – Cumulative Test (after 2–3 vowels)

Optional.

  • Mix of pictures from multiple vowels (e.g., Short A + Short I + Short E)
  • 25–30 questions
  • Passing score: maximum 6 mistakes
  • Purpose: ensure the student retains all previous material

Step 8 – Ready for the next series

When the student passes all required tests, they are ready for:

  • Next vowel book (Short A → Short I → Short E → Short O → Short U), or
  • Next phonics series (Grade 1) which includes IPA and more complex patterns.

Notes for Teachers

1. Do not explain grammar. Just say the word clearly. Let the student listen and repeat.

2. Do not force memorization. Repetition across units will do the work naturally.

3. Keep it short and happy. 7 units per book is enough. If the student is tired, stop. Coloring is a reward, not a task.
